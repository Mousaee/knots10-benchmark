\begin{abstract}
Knot classification is fundamental to surgical training, robotic manipulation, and industrial safety. While machine learning has been applied to mathematical knot diagrams, no prior study has systematically evaluated fine-grained visual classification of physical knots from real-world photographs under a standardized benchmark protocol. We present Knots-10, a benchmark built on the 10Knots dataset (1,440 images, 10 knot types) with a tightness-stratified evaluation protocol that trains on loosely tied knots and tests on tightly dressed ones. Beyond high classification accuracy (up to 99.4\% with Swin-T), we demonstrate a statistically significant alignment between visual confusion structure and knot-theoretic similarity (Spearman $\rho$ up to $-0.49$, $p < 0.001$), and show that topology-guided training reshapes embedding geometry to reflect domain knowledge. Specifically, we propose topology-aware centroid alignment (TACA), which yields a dual benefit: (i) a 1.25 percentage-point accuracy improvement on ResNet-18 (95.83\% $\to$ 97.08\%) and (ii) a 40\% increase in embedding--topology alignment (Spearman $\rho$: $0.46 \to 0.65$, $p < 0.001$), with t-SNE and UMAP visualizations confirming family-level clustering. The evaluation code and trained models will be publicly released.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} knot classification, fine-grained recognition, benchmark dataset, topological analysis, topology-guided learning, representation alignment

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Knots are among the oldest and most ubiquitous human technologies. From surgical suturing \citep{wang2018satr} to maritime rigging, rock climbing \citep{luebben2004}, and industrial load securing, the ability to tie and identify specific knot types remains a critical practical skill. Incorrect knot selection can lead to catastrophic failures---a bowline mistaken for a slip knot under load, or an improperly dressed reef knot in a surgical context. Yet despite centuries of practical use, knot identification still relies primarily on human expertise, and automated fine-grained classification of physical knot types remains an open problem.

Recent work at the intersection of machine learning and knot theory has made progress on \emph{mathematical} knot classification: predicting invariants from planar diagrams \citep{knotdetection2025, hughes2020neural, craven2024illuminating}, or learning topological properties from abstract representations \citep{gukov2024rigor, grunbaum2022narrowing}. In robotics, knot detection has been limited to binary classification---knotted versus unknotted---for rope manipulation tasks \citep{sundaresan2020, grannen2020}. However, to the best of our knowledge, no prior work has addressed the fine-grained classification of physical knot \emph{types} from real-world photographs under a standardized evaluation protocol. This gap is significant: while mathematical knot diagrams are clean, standardized inputs, physical knots exhibit high intra-class variability due to rope material, tightness, lighting, and viewing angle.

This paper bridges the gap between mathematical knot theory and practical visual recognition. We build upon the publicly available 10Knots dataset \citep{cameron2018tenknots}, which provides raw photographs of physical knots but lacks a standardized evaluation protocol, topological annotations, or baseline results. Our contribution is not the images themselves, but the benchmark framework we construct around them: a tightness-stratified evaluation protocol that creates a realistic domain shift, a topological annotation scheme linking each knot type to its mathematical properties, and a novel topology-guided training approach grounded in knot theory.

Specifically, we make four contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Problem formulation.} We formalize physical knot recognition as a fine-grained visual classification (FGVC) task, identifying its unique challenges: high intra-class variability from tightness and lighting variation, subtle inter-class differences rooted in topological structure, and a natural domain shift between training and deployment conditions.

    \item \textbf{Knots-10 benchmark.} We organize the 10Knots dataset \citep{cameron2018tenknots} into a reproducible benchmark with a tightness-stratified evaluation protocol and provide baselines from five architectures spanning CNNs and transformers.

    \item \textbf{Topological difficulty analysis.} We propose a quantitative framework linking knot-theoretic properties (crossing number, composite structure, family membership) to visual classification difficulty, and demonstrate that pairwise confusion rates are significantly correlated with topological distance.

    \item \textbf{Topology-guided representation learning.} We introduce a topology-aware regularization loss that aligns learned feature representations with knot-theoretic structure, improving both classification accuracy and embedding quality.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsubsection{Machine Learning for Mathematical Knot Theory}

Classical knot classification relies on computing invariants such as crossing number, the Alexander polynomial, and the Jones polynomial from planar diagrams \citep{adams2004}. Recently, deep learning has entered this domain. \citet{knotdetection2025} combined CNNs and transformers with Jones polynomial computation to classify knot diagrams from images. \citet{hughes2020neural} introduced neural networks for predicting knot invariants such as the slice genus and the Ozsv\'{a}th--Szab\'{o} $\tau$-invariant, establishing a foundational data-driven approach to knot theory. \citet{craven2024illuminating} used neural networks to discover relations between knot invariants, while \citet{grunbaum2022narrowing} applied deep learning to bridge combinatorial and hyperbolic invariants. \citet{gukov2024rigor} surveyed rigorous methods for applying machine learning to topology and pure mathematics. All these works operate on idealized mathematical representations rather than photographs of physical rope knots.


\subsubsection{Robotic Rope Manipulation and Knot Detection}

In robotics, knot detection appears in the context of deformable linear object (DLO) manipulation. \citet{matsuno2005manipulation} pioneered the use of knot invariants to classify topological states of manipulated ropes. \citet{tiebot2024} demonstrated learning to tie a necktie via a real-to-sim-to-real pipeline. \citet{sundaresan2020} and \citet{grannen2020} addressed binary knot detection for robotic untangling but did not attempt fine-grained classification among knot types. Surgical knot assessment \citep{moorthy2004, vedula2016, wang2018satr} evaluates tying technique rather than knot identity.


\subsubsection{Fine-Grained Visual Classification}

Our task is closely related to fine-grained visual classification (FGVC), where the goal is to distinguish visually similar subcategories \citep{wei2022fgia}. Benchmark tasks include bird species recognition \citep{wah2011cub}, car model identification \citep{krause2013}, and plant disease detection \citep{mohanty2016}. In industrial inspection, \citet{gao2021blnn} achieved 99.2\% accuracy on wood knot defect classification using bilinear CNNs. Transfer learning from ImageNet-pretrained models has become the dominant paradigm for FGVC \citep{he2016resnet, dosovitskiy2021vit, zhuang2021transfer}.


\subsubsection{Domain Shift in Visual Recognition}

A distinctive feature of our benchmark is the deliberate domain shift between training and test sets. This is analogous to domain adaptation and domain generalization scenarios \citep{ben-david2010, ganin2016, zhou2022domain}, where distribution mismatch degrades performance. Our tightness-stratified protocol provides a controlled setting to study how well pretrained features transfer across physical deformation states.

\subsubsection{Structure-Aware Representation Learning}

Several lines of work have explored injecting domain-specific structural priors into learned representations. Label hierarchy-aware losses \citep{barz2019hierarchy} use taxonomic distances to smooth class targets, encouraging semantically related classes to share similar embeddings. Supervised contrastive learning \citep{khosla2020supcon} pulls same-class samples together while pushing different-class samples apart. \citet{wu2020topological} demonstrated that topological regularization can improve the structure of learned feature spaces. Structured losses that respect class relationships have shown benefits for few-shot generalization \citep{snell2017prototypical}, though careful evaluation is needed to validate embedding improvements \citep{musgrave2020reality}. Our topology-guided training extends this paradigm to knot theory, using knot-theoretic distances as a domain-specific structural prior.

\subsection{Topological Difficulty Analysis}\label{sec:topo_difficulty}

A central hypothesis of this work is that the difficulty of visually distinguishing two knot types is related to their topological similarity. Using the topological distance metric defined in Section~\ref{sec:topo_metric}, we validate this intuition through difficulty tier analysis and empirical correlation with confusion rates.

\subsubsection{Difficulty Tiers}

Based on the topological properties (Table~\ref{tab:knot_taxonomy}), we partition the 10 classes into three difficulty tiers (Figure~\ref{fig:difficulty}):

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Easy}: CH, ABK, BK---structurally distinct families with low crossing numbers or unique visual signatures.
    \item \textbf{Medium}: F8K, F8L, OHK, SK---share the trefoil or figure-eight base structure; moderate visual overlap.
    \item \textbf{Hard}: RK, FSK, FMB---composite knots with complex crossing patterns and similar traced structures.
\end{itemize}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{difficulty_tiers.pdf}
\caption{Knot difficulty tiers based on topological properties. Bar length indicates visual crossing number.}
\label{fig:difficulty}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Weight Sensitivity}

To verify that our results are robust to the choice of distance metric weights (Section~\ref{sec:topo_metric}), we conducted a Monte Carlo sensitivity analysis. We perturbed each weight by up to $\pm 50\%$ and re-normalized to sum to 1.0, generating 200 random weight vectors. The resulting Spearman correlations (on EfficientNet-B0) have mean $\rho = -0.483 \pm 0.016$, with 100\% of perturbations yielding $\rho < -0.25$. Across all five models, switching from the default to uniform weights ($w_f = 0.2$) changes $\rho$ by less than 0.01. Only extreme single-factor configurations (e.g., family membership alone) degrade the correlation to non-significance. These results indicate that the topological--visual correlation is a robust phenomenon, not an artifact of a particular weight choice (Figure~\ref{fig:weight_sensitivity}).

\subsection{Topological-Visual Correlation Analysis}\label{sec:correlation}

We now validate the central hypothesis: that topological similarity predicts visual confusion. For each model, we extract the pairwise confusion rate $c(k_i, k_j)$ from the normalized confusion matrix and compute its correlation with the topological distance $d(k_i, k_j)$.

\subsubsection{Correlation Results}

Table~\ref{tab:correlation} reports Spearman and Pearson correlations between topological distance and pairwise confusion rate across all 45 knot pairs.

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\caption{Correlation between topological distance and pairwise confusion rate. Negative correlation indicates that topologically similar knots are more frequently confused, supporting alignment between visual confusion and knot-theoretic structure.}
\label{tab:correlation}
\small
\begin{tabular}{@{}lcccc@{}}
\toprule
Model & Spearman $\rho$ & $p$-value & Pearson $r$ & $p$-value \\
\midrule
ResNet-18 & $-0.291$ & 0.052 & $-0.301$ & 0.044 \\
ResNet-50 & $-0.472$ & 0.001 & $-0.453$ & 0.002 \\
EfficientNet-B0 & $-0.491$ & $<0.001$ & $-0.426$ & 0.004 \\
ViT-B/16 & $-0.398$ & 0.007 & $-0.359$ & 0.015 \\
Swin-T & $-0.214$ & 0.158 & $-0.195$ & 0.198 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Three of five models show statistically significant negative Spearman correlations at $p < 0.05$, with ResNet-18 marginally significant ($\rho = -0.291$, $p = 0.052$). The strongest correlation appears in EfficientNet-B0 ($\rho = -0.491$, $p < 0.001$), followed by ResNet-50 ($\rho = -0.472$, $p = 0.001$). The exception is Swin-T: as it produces extremely sparse confusion patterns (only 3 non-zero off-diagonal pairs out of 45), the correlation estimate becomes statistically underpowered.

\noindent\textbf{Note on statistical independence.} Since the 45 pairwise distances are derived from a symmetric distance matrix and a shared confusion matrix, they are not fully independent observations. The reported $p$-values should therefore be interpreted as descriptive indicators of association strength rather than strict inferential statistics.

\subsubsection{Interpretation}

This result yields a clear interpretation: \emph{weaker models' errors are structured by knot theory, while stronger models learn to transcend topological similarity}. The progression from ResNet-18 ($\rho = -0.291$) through EfficientNet-B0 ($\rho = -0.491$) to Swin-T ($\rho = -0.214$, n.s.) does not reflect a monotonic trend in correlation strength, but rather a transition from noisy errors (ResNet-18 makes enough errors but some are random) through topology-aligned errors (EfficientNet-B0 exhibits the strongest alignment between confusion structure and topological similarity) to near-elimination of errors (Swin-T).

The most confused pairs across models are consistent with topological predictions:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{F8K--OHK} ($d = 0.156$): Both derive from low-crossing prime knots ($4_1$ and $3_1$), sharing a single-loop stopper structure.
    \item \textbf{F8K--SK} ($d = 0.231$): SK is a slip variant of the trefoil, visually similar to F8K when tightly dressed.
    \item \textbf{FSK--FMB} ($d = 0.188$): Both are composite bend knots with intertwined rope segments.
\end{itemize}

Figure~\ref{fig:confusion} visualizes confusion matrices for the best-performing model (Swin-T) and a representative weaker model (ViT-B/16), illustrating how topological similarity manifests in systematic confusion patterns.

ViT-B/16 shows systematic confusion between topologically similar knots: Figure-8 Knot vs.\ Overhand Knot (both prime stopper knots) and Fisherman's Knot vs.\ Flemish Bend (both composite bends). \subsubsection{Grad-CAM Visualization}
\label{sec:gradcam}

To verify that models attend to knot-relevant image regions rather than background artifacts, we apply Gradient-weighted Class Activation Mapping (Grad-CAM) \citep{selvaraju2017gradcam} to ResNet-18 and ResNet-50. For our transformer-based models, attention-based interpretability methods \citep{chefer2021transformer} offer complementary insights. Figure~\ref{fig:gradcam} shows activation heatmaps for one test image per class.

Both models consistently focus on the central knot structure, confirming that classification decisions are driven by rope crossing patterns rather than contextual cues such as the background surface. However, two notable differences emerge:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Spatial precision.} ResNet-50 produces tighter, more concentrated activation regions than ResNet-18, consistent with its deeper feature hierarchy enabling finer spatial discrimination.
    \item \textbf{Contextual leakage.} For Clove Hitch, ResNet-18's activation extends along the vertical pole, suggesting partial reliance on the mounting object rather than the knot topology itself. ResNet-50 shows less of this effect.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Topology-Guided Training Results}\label{sec:topo_results}

The correlation analysis in Section~\ref{sec:correlation} establishes that topological distance predicts visual confusion. A natural question follows: can we exploit this topological prior during training to induce more structured representations? We apply topology-guided training (see Section~\ref{sec:topo_method} for the method definition) and evaluate its impact on both classification accuracy and embedding quality.

\subsubsection{Classification Results}

Table~\ref{tab:topo_acc} compares test accuracy between baseline (cross-entropy only) and topology-guided training (CE+TACA). On ResNet-18, topology-guided training improves test accuracy by 1.25 percentage points (95.83\% $\to$ 97.08\%). For ResNet-50, the difference is negligible (96.04\% $\to$ 95.83\%, a 0.21 percentage-point decrease). Given 480 test samples, the 95\% confidence interval for a 96\% accuracy is approximately $\pm$1.8\% (standard error $\approx \sqrt{p(1-p)/n} \approx 0.009$), indicating that this difference falls well within sampling variability. This capacity-dependent effect is consistent with prior findings that regularization benefits diminish for higher-capacity models on small datasets \citep{zhuang2021transfer}.

These results establish TACA as the effective component of topology-guided training.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Architecture Selection for Small-Scale FGVC}

Our results counter the intuition that larger models necessarily yield better fine-grained performance. ViT-B/16, despite having 85.8M parameters (3$\times$ Swin-T), achieves only 95.2\% test accuracy. This confirms findings from the broader FGVC literature that pure transformers require large-scale data to outperform CNNs \citep{dosovitskiy2021vit}.

Swin-T's advantage is attributable to its hierarchical design: shifted windows provide local attention at early layers (analogous to CNN convolutions) while enabling cross-window information flow at deeper layers. For physical knot classification, this hybrid strategy captures both fine-grained rope crossing patterns and global knot topology.

\subsection{The Domain Shift Challenge}

The 3--5\% accuracy gap between validation and test sets across four of five models (all except Swin-T, which shows only a 0.6\% gap) quantifies the domain shift induced by tightness variation. Tightly dressed knots compress crossing structures, reduce visible rope segments, and alter shadow patterns. That all models still exceed 95\% suggests ImageNet-pretrained features are reasonably robust to this deformation, but the residual gap indicates room for domain adaptation techniques.

\subsection{Topological Priors for Error Analysis}

The topological difficulty analysis offers practical value beyond academic interest. By identifying which knot pairs are inherently confusable, practitioners can design targeted data augmentation for hard pairs, implement hierarchical classifiers, and establish realistic performance expectations.

\subsection{Topology-Guided Training: Beyond Accuracy}

Given that Swin-T achieves 99.4\% test accuracy, standard classification accuracy has reached near-saturation on this benchmark. Our ablation study reveals that topology-aware centroid alignment (TACA) provides a measurable 1.25-point accuracy gain on ResNet-18, but the more important finding lies in deeper dimensions: representation structure, embedding interpretability, and alignment with domain knowledge. This shift in evaluation focus is essential for benchmarks approaching performance ceilings, where accuracy alone becomes an insufficient metric.

Our embedding alignment results reinforce this perspective. Topology-guided training both improves accuracy and fundamentally restructures the learned representation space. The 40\% improvement in embedding-topology correlation for ResNet-18 (Spearman $\rho$: $0.46 \to 0.65$, $p < 0.001$) demonstrates that domain-specific structural priors can enhance both classification performance and representation quality. This finding resonates with recent work on representation quality metrics \citep{kornblith2019similarity} and suggests practical value for downstream tasks such as few-shot learning, out-of-distribution detection, and human-interpretable feature spaces.

The improvement is particularly notable given that the topological distance metric uses hand-crafted weights. Learning these weights jointly with the model could further improve alignment.

\subsection{Limitations}

Several limitations should be acknowledged. First, the dataset uses a single rope type (blue synthetic climbing rope); generalization to different materials, colors, and diameters remains untested, which limits claims about real-world deployment. Second, our topological distance metric uses hand-crafted weights that assume roughly equal contributions from each factor; learning these weights from data could improve correlation. Third, the dataset size (1{,}440 images) is modest by modern standards; this benchmark is intended as a controlled foundational study establishing the link between knot theory and visual recognition, rather than a large-scale industrial dataset. Fourth, the tightness-stratified domain shift, while realistic, represents a relatively controlled form of distribution mismatch; deployment scenarios may involve more severe variations such as occlusion, background clutter, or mixed rope materials. Fifth, topology-guided training was evaluated only on ResNet-18 and ResNet-50; its effect on Swin-T was not tested because the near-perfect baseline accuracy (99.4\%) leaves insufficient room for improvement, though the representation-level benefits may still apply.

\subsection{Summary and Outlook}

We have presented Knots-10, a benchmark for fine-grained visual classification of physical knots, with a tightness-stratified evaluation protocol that creates a realistic domain shift. Among five baseline architectures, Swin-T achieves 99.4\% test accuracy.

Our topological difficulty analysis reveals a statistically significant link between knot-theoretic similarity and visual confusion rates. Building on this insight, we proposed topology-guided training, which regularizes the learned embedding space to align with knot-theoretic structure. Ablation experiments demonstrate that topology-aware centroid alignment (TACA) improves ResNet-18 accuracy by 1.25 percentage points (95.83\% $\to$ 97.08\%), and embedding analysis reveals a substantial improvement in representation quality: Spearman correlation between class centroid distances and topological distances increases by up to 40\% ($0.46 \to 0.65$, $p < 0.001$). Visualization confirms that topology-guided models learn family-coherent embedding spaces where topologically related knots cluster together.

Future work will extend the dataset to additional knot types and rope materials---particularly varying colors, diameters, and material properties (e.g., natural fiber vs.\ synthetic)---to evaluate generalization under cross-material domain shift. Other directions include learning the topological distance weights jointly with the model, investigating whether topology-aware representations improve few-shot generalization to novel knot types, and scaling to larger knot inventories with more complex topological structure.

\section{Methods}\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{The Knots-10 Dataset}\label{sec:dataset}

\subsubsection{Dataset Overview}

\noindent\textbf{Remark.} The crossing numbers ($C_{\text{vis}}$) in Table~\ref{tab:knot_taxonomy} reflect the visual crossing structure of each practical knot type, not formal mathematical knot invariants. Knots such as the Bowline, Alpine Butterfly, and Clove Hitch do not have standard crossing numbers in mathematical knot theory; the values listed are estimates based on their physical appearance when tied.

\subsubsection{Image Acquisition Protocol}

Each knot was photographed under a factorial design of controlled variables \citep{cameron2018tenknots}:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Lighting} (3 levels): Diffuse light (DL), spotlight from above (SLA), and spotlight from the side (SLS).
    \item \textbf{Tightness} (3 levels): Set (tightly dressed), Loose (moderately slack), and VeryLoose (maximally slack).
    \item \textbf{Instance variation}: 16 unique instances per lighting-tightness combination, capturing variation in orientation and placement.
\end{itemize}

This yields 144 images per knot class and 1{,}440 images in total. All images feature climbing rope photographed from a top-down perspective.

\subsubsection{Tightness-Stratified Evaluation Protocol}

Rather than a random split, we adopt a \emph{tightness-stratified} partition:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Training}: Loose + VeryLoose images (960 total; 768 after holdout).
    \item \textbf{Validation}: 20\% stratified holdout from training (192 images).
    \item \textbf{Test}: All Set (tightly dressed) images (480 images).
\end{itemize}

This design is motivated by deployment realism: in practice, knots are encountered in their dressed state, while training data may come from loosely tied demonstrations. The resulting domain shift---from slack to taut rope geometry---tests whether models learn genuine structural features rather than superficial texture cues.


\subsection{Topological Distance Metric}
\label{sec:topo_metric}

We define a pairwise topological distance $d(k_i, k_j)$ between knot classes as a weighted combination of five factors:

\begin{equation}
d(k_i, k_j) = \sum_{f=1}^{5} w_f \cdot \delta_f(k_i, k_j)
\end{equation}

\noindent where the factors are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\delta_1$: Normalized crossing number difference, $|C_{\text{vis}}(k_i) - C_{\text{vis}}(k_j)| / 8$.
    \item $\delta_2$: Family membership (0 if same family, 1 otherwise).
    \item $\delta_3$: Type similarity (0 if same type, 0.5 otherwise). The intermediate value 0.5 reflects partial similarity between different knot types while preserving a graded distance scale, avoiding the hard binary separation imposed by $\delta_2$.
    \item $\delta_4$: Component count difference, $|n_{\text{comp}}(k_i) - n_{\text{comp}}(k_j)|$, where $n_{\text{comp}}$ is the number of constituent knots (1 for prime knots, 2 for composite knots).
    \item $\delta_5$: Structural derivation penalty, encoding known relationships (e.g., OHK and SK both derive from the trefoil $3_1$; F8K and FMB share the $4_1$ structure).
\end{itemize}
\noindent The weights are set to $w_1 = 0.25$ (crossing number), $w_2 = 0.25$ (family), $w_3 = 0.15$ (type), $w_4 = 0.10$ (components), $w_5 = 0.25$ (structural derivation), summing to 1.0. These values reflect the relative importance of crossing number, family membership, and structural derivation as primary factors, with type similarity and component count as secondary factors.


\subsection{Model Architectures}
\label{sec:models}

We evaluate five representative architectures spanning CNNs and transformers:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{ResNet-18} (11.2M params): Lightweight CNN baseline \citep{he2016resnet}.
    \item \textbf{ResNet-50} (23.5M): Deeper CNN with bottleneck blocks.
    \item \textbf{EfficientNet-B0} (4.0M): Compound-scaled CNN \citep{tan2019efficientnet}.
    \item \textbf{ViT-B/16} (85.8M): Standard Vision Transformer \citep{dosovitskiy2021vit}.
    \item \textbf{Swin-T} (27.5M): Hierarchical vision transformer with shifted windows \citep{liu2021swin}.
\end{itemize}

All models are initialized with ImageNet-pretrained weights and fine-tuned end-to-end.


\subsection{Training Protocol}
\label{sec:training}

All models are trained for 20 epochs using AdamW ($\text{lr}=10^{-4}$, weight decay $10^{-4}$) with cosine annealing. Input images are resized to $224 \times 224$. Training augmentations include random horizontal flip, rotation ($\pm 15^{\circ}$), and color jitter (brightness=0.2, contrast=0.2, saturation=0.2, hue=0.1). Batch size is 32. The best checkpoint by validation accuracy is selected for test evaluation. All experiments use a single Apple M-series processor via the MPS backend in PyTorch 2.9. Due to the deterministic nature of the tightness-stratified split and the limited dataset size, all experiments were conducted with a fixed random seed (seed = 42) to ensure exact reproducibility.


\subsection{Topology-Guided Training}
\label{sec:topo_method}

Given the topological distance matrix $\mathbf{D}^{\text{topo}} \in \mathbb{R}^{K \times K}$ defined in Section~\ref{sec:topo_metric}, we add regularization terms to the standard cross-entropy loss. Let $\mathbf{z}_i$ denote the penultimate-layer representation (after global average pooling) for sample $i$, and let $\boldsymbol{\mu}_k = \frac{1}{|S_k|}\sum_{i \in S_k} \mathbf{z}_i$ be the class centroid for class $k$ within a mini-batch $S_k$.

\subsubsection{Topology-Aware Centroid Alignment (TACA)}

The TACA loss encourages the pairwise distances between class centroids in embedding space to match the topological distance matrix:

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{TACA}} = \frac{1}{K^2}\left\|\hat{\mathbf{D}}^{\text{emb}} - \mathbf{D}^{\text{topo}}\right\|_F^2
\end{equation}
where $\hat{\mathbf{D}}^{\text{emb}}_{k,k'} = \|\boldsymbol{\mu}_k - \boldsymbol{\mu}_{k'}\|_2 / \max_{i,j}\|\boldsymbol{\mu}_i - \boldsymbol{\mu}_j\|_2$ is the embedding distance between class centroids, normalized by the maximum centroid distance to ensure comparable scale with the topological distance matrix. This loss directly aligns the geometry of the learned embedding space with the knot-theoretic structure. To ensure stable centroid estimation, $\boldsymbol{\mu}_k$ is computed using all samples of class $k$ present in the mini-batch; if a class is absent from a batch, its contribution to $\mathcal{L}_{\text{TACA}}$ for that batch is omitted.

\subsubsection{Topology-Aware Margin Loss (TAML)}

The TAML loss is a margin-based contrastive term that uses topological distance to set per-pair margins, pushing samples away from centroids of topologically similar but different classes (hard negatives):

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{TAML}} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{P}|}\sum_{(i,j) \in \mathcal{P}} \max\left(0,\; \text{sim}(\hat{\mathbf{z}}_i, \hat{\mathbf{z}}_j) - (1 - d^{\text{topo}}_{y_i, y_j})\right)
\end{equation}
where $\hat{\mathbf{z}}_i = \mathbf{z}_i / \|\mathbf{z}_i\|_2$ is the $L_2$-normalized embedding, $\text{sim}(\cdot, \cdot)$ denotes cosine similarity, $\mathcal{P} = \{(i,j) : y_i \neq y_j\}$ is the set of cross-class sample pairs in the mini-batch, and $d^{\text{topo}}_{y_i, y_j}$ is the topological distance between their respective classes. The term $(1 - d^{\text{topo}}_{y_i, y_j})$ sets a class-pair-specific similarity threshold: topologically distant classes are allowed higher similarity without penalty, while topologically similar classes must be pushed further apart.

\subsubsection{Combined Loss}

The total loss is:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{CE}} + \lambda_{\text{TACA}} \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{TACA}} + \lambda_{\text{TAML}} \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{TAML}}
\end{equation}
with $\lambda_{\text{TACA}} = 0.1$ and $\lambda_{\text{TAML}} = 0.005$ in all experiments. The ablation study (Table~\ref{tab:loss_ablation}) evaluates each component independently.
